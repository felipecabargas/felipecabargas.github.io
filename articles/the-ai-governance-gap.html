The AI Governance Gap No One Is Talking About

AI Governance

Most companies writing AI governance policies today are performing compliance, not practicing it. There's a gap — wide and growing — between what organizations say they're doing with AI and what they're actually doing. And it's not a gap that more frameworks will fix.

A governance document that lives in Confluence and never informs a product decision is not governance. It's paperwork.

Why compliance theater is so attractive

The incentives are misaligned. Legal teams want documentation. Boards want to be able to say they have a policy. Sales teams want a PDF to send to enterprise procurement. None of these stakeholders are primarily worried about whether the policy actually changes behavior on the ground.

So companies optimize for the artifact — the policy document, the ethics review checklist, the responsible AI principles page — rather than the outcome: AI that behaves predictably, fairly, and in accordance with their stated values.

What real governance looks like

The companies that get this right treat governance as a product problem, not a compliance problem. They ask: how does this policy get enforced at the point where a decision is made? Not in a quarterly review, not in a legal audit — but when an engineer is choosing a training dataset or a PM is deciding whether to ship a feature.

Real governance is operational. It lives in pull request templates, in feature flag policies, in model evaluation checklists that gate deployment. It's not a document — it's a set of defaults that make the right thing the easy thing.

Three things that actually work

Make governance legible to builders. Translate your policy into concrete, actionable criteria that engineers and PMs can apply without a lawyer in the room. If someone has to ask "does this apply to me?" the policy has failed.

Instrument the gap. If you can't measure whether your governance is working, it isn't. What's the error rate on your flagging system? How many model deployments triggered a risk review last quarter? The absence of these metrics is itself a signal.

Treat incidents as governance failures, not bad luck. When an AI system behaves unexpectedly, the first question shouldn't be "how do we fix this?" It should be "what in our governance process failed to catch this?" Work backwards from incidents to the policy gap that allowed them.

The companies that get AI governance right aren't the ones with the longest policy documents. They're the ones where the question "is this the right thing to do?" gets asked — and answered — before deployment, not after.
